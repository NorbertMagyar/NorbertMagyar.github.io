Gradient-Driven Logistic Cellular Automata


      I have always been fascinated by emergence. How can simple, repetitive processes between a number of entities lead to such complexities as we observe in nature. The best example being biological life itself, which arises from the electromagnetic interaction of charged elementary particles in large numbers. Within the digital realm, Conway's Game of Life is a great example of emergence. Although cells live or die according to very simple rules within the game, these simple rules are sufficient to make the game Turing complete! Meaning that you can devise any computation within the game itself, for example generate the Game of Life within the Game of Life, or in other words, simulate itself! Even a complete computer has been recreated in the Game of Life.

Arguably the simplest computation, or recurrence relation, that can lead to surprisingly complex and chaotic behaviour is the logistic map. While reading up on both the logistic map and Conway's Game of Life a couple of years back, the idea came to me that maybe I could combine both into a Game of Life-like cellular automata. Cellular automata are basically a set of rules that are applied iteratively to a grid of cells that can be in various states, either discrete or continuous. I thought of replacing the dead-or-alive two-state cells in the Game of Life with a continuously varying state in which neighbouring cells somehow evolve according to the logistic map. After some tweaking, I came up with a 2D cellular-automata-style simulation on a periodic grid, with the update law based on the logistic map, as mentioned, where the local growth parameter r is modulated by the spatial gradient between neighboring cells.

The cell update is: \[ A_{t+1}(i,j) = A_t(i,j)\,\bigl(c_a + c_b\,f_t(i,j)\bigr)\,\bigl(1 - A_t(i,j)\bigr) \] where the default factor is based on central-difference gradients in both axes, e.g. \[ f_t(i,j) = \partial_x A_t(i,j) + \partial_y A_t(i,j). \]

The grid is initialized with 0.5 plus small random Gaussian fluctuations. The individual cell values are always between 0 and 1, as in the case of the logistic map.

For different values of ca and cb, the simulation produces very different emergent patterns and dynamics: coherent structures, vortex-like shapes, fronts, and strongly chaotic textures. I was amazed to see the complexity that arises for various values for the two constants, and I spent a couple of hours exploring the parameter space. Below you can see animations of some of the most interesting simulations, for a couple of thousand iterations.

Whether there is some deeper logical substrate, meaning, or connection to other areas of mathematics or physics behind these various dynamics, I couldn't figure out yet. As is the case with most nonlinear toy models, there probably is. For now, it's art!

The repository with the script can be found on my GitHub. It uses GPU acceleration (via CuPy) when available. That came in quite handy when exploring the parameter space, even with my modest NVIDIA T600 laptop GPU.

Enjoy!
