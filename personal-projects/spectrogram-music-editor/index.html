Spectrogram Music Editor

This is a browser-based music sketchpad built around a simple idea: paint sound directly on a time-frequency plane and listen to the result. The vertical axis represents frequency, the horizontal axis represents time, and brightness maps to amplitude. Draw a diagonal line and you get a sweep. Paint several bands and you get chords, textures, and noisy clouds.

The app supports several paint-like tools, keyboard shortcuts, and in-browser playback. Everything runs locally in JavaScript through the Web Audio API, so nothing needs to be installed.

How the synthesizer works

The editor stores a painted intensity field on a time-frequency grid. You can think of it as a hand-drawn magnitude map \(A(t, f)\), where horizontal position is time \(t\), vertical position is frequency \(f\), and brightness is amplitude. If you draw a narrow ridge that moves upward in frequency, the synth interprets that geometry as a gliding tone. If you paint several ridges at once, it can become a chord or a polyphonic texture. If you spray or blur energy over a wide band, it behaves more like noise or a diffuse cloud.

The central DSP difficulty is that the picture does not directly contain full waveform information. A sound waveform is a pressure signal \(x(t)\), but the painted canvas mostly describes magnitudes in the time-frequency plane. In simplified form, a sinusoidal reconstruction looks like

\[ x(t)=\sum_k a_k(t)\sin\!\left(2\pi\int_0^t f_k(\tau)\,d\tau+\phi_{k,0}\right), \]

where \(a_k(t)\) is the amplitude of a partial, \(f_k(t)\) is its instantaneous frequency, and \(\phi_{k,0}\) is an initial phase. Equivalently, one can write the same idea in terms of a running phase \(\phi_k(t)\), with frequency recovered from

\[ f_k(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_k}{dt}. \]

The amplitudes are suggested by the drawing, but the phases are not painted explicitly. That missing phase information is why spectrogram inversion is interesting: many different waveforms can share a similar-looking magnitude image.

This app therefore exposes several render philosophies rather than pretending there is one unique inverse. Some modes treat drawn structures as physical sound objects: continuous ridges become oscillators with continuous phase, sharp vertical marks behave like transients, and fuzzy paint becomes a more decorrelated residual layer. Other modes ignore geometry and reconstruct the image more literally, for example by treating each frequency bin independently or by iteratively solving a phase-retrieval problem in an STFT representation.

In the frequency domain, the classic short-time Fourier transform writes local spectra as \(X(m, k)\) for frame \(m\) and bin \(k\). The painted canvas mainly specifies something analogous to the magnitude \(\lvert X(m, k)\rvert\), while the missing phase still has to be inferred.

\[ X(m,k)=\lvert X(m,k)\rvert e^{i\,\arg X(m,k)}. \]

Griffin-Lim then searches for phases \(\arg X(m, k)\) that are consistent with a real time-domain signal. By contrast, the geometry-based renderer does not solve the inverse problem blindly: it infers likely physical phase behavior from the shapes you paint.

Render modes

Geometry coherence. This is the most object-oriented renderer. It tracks continuous painted ridges, estimates whether they are coherent or noisy, applies deterministic phase continuity along stable tracks, resets phase around transient-like onsets, and adds a residual layer for broad or fuzzy energy. This usually sounds the most physically organized for lines, glides, harmonic stacks, and articulated gestures.

Independent oscillators. This mode still detects ridges, but then treats them as unrelated oscillators. Each track gets its own deterministic initial phase and evolves independently, without harmonic grouping, transient-driven phase resets, or diffusion logic. The result is often cleaner than raw spectrogram resynthesis, but less lifelike and less coupled than geometry coherence.

Spectral bins. This is close to classic additive spectrogram synthesis. Each frequency row acts like its own oscillator with its own phase history, and the picture is followed more literally as a bin amplitude image. This can preserve the look of the drawing quite faithfully, but it often sounds buzzy, smeared, or metallic because neighboring bins are not explicitly coordinated into physical objects.

Griffin-Lim. This mode converts the drawing into a target magnitude spectrogram and then runs iterative STFT phase reconstruction. The algorithm alternates between inverse STFT, forward STFT, and magnitude replacement until the phases become more self-consistent. It is mathematically closer to "invert the painted spectrogram" than the oscillator-based modes, but it can still sound phasey or metallic, especially on sharp transients or sparse tonal structures.

Hybrid coherence + Griffin-Lim. This starts from the geometry-coherence renderer, computes an STFT of that audio, and uses its phases as initialization for a shorter Griffin-Lim refinement. Conceptually, it asks whether geometry-derived phase can provide a better starting guess than a generic deterministic phase field. In practice it often sits between the two worlds: more image-faithful than pure geometry synthesis, but more stable and physically organized than Griffin-Lim from scratch.

The diagnostics overlay is there for the same reason: this page is meant to be a synthesis instrument and a DSP playground at the same time. Coherent tracks, noisy regions, harmonic groupings, and phase-reset locations are all visual clues about how the renderer is interpreting your drawing rather than merely replaying pixels.
